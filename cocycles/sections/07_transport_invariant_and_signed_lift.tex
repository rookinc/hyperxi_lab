\section{Transport Invariant and Signed Lift}

We now identify the binary transport invariant governing the lift
\[
G_{30} \to G_{15}.
\]
The key point is that the sign on a core edge is not assigned ad hoc:
it is induced by the actual lifted transport and admits a canonical
geometric interpretation on the dodecahedron.

\subsection{The antipodal lift}

The graph $G_{30}$ is a $2$-fold cover of $G_{15}$. Thus each vertex
$x \in V(G_{15})$ has a two-point fiber
\[
\pi^{-1}(x)=\{x^+,x^-\}\subset V(G_{30}).
\]
For an edge $e=\{x,y\}\in E(G_{15})$, the adjacency upstairs connects the
two fibers $\pi^{-1}(x)$ and $\pi^{-1}(y)$ in exactly one of two ways:
either sheet is preserved, or sheet is exchanged. This binary distinction
defines the transport parity of the edge.

\subsection{Canonical dodecahedral model of the core}

By the identification
\[
G_{15}\cong L(\mathrm{Petersen}),
\]
we may regard the vertices of $G_{15}$ as the $15$ opposite-edge classes
of the dodecahedron. Each vertex of $G_{30}$ then corresponds to one of
the two actual dodecahedral edges in the corresponding opposite-edge class.

Let $e,f$ be two dodecahedral edges arising from adjacent vertices of
$G_{15}$. Their relation is classified as follows:

\begin{itemize}
\item \emph{local} if $e$ and $f$ share a vertex and lie on a common face;
\item \emph{nonlocal} if they neither share a vertex nor lie on a common face,
and are not opposite edges.
\end{itemize}

Empirically, when one examines the two lifted edges in $G_{30}$ above a
single edge of $G_{15}$, only two patterns occur:
\[
(LL)\qquad\text{or}\qquad(NN).
\]

\subsection{Definition of the Thalean cocycle}

\begin{definition}[Thalean cocycle]
Let $e=\{x,y\}\in E(G_{15})$. Consider the two lifted edges in $G_{30}$
connecting the fibers $\pi^{-1}(x)$ and $\pi^{-1}(y)$, and push them to
the canonical opposite-edge model of the dodecahedron. Define
\[
\varepsilon(e)\in \mathbb{Z}_2
\]
by
\[
\varepsilon(e)=
\begin{cases}
0,&\text{if the lifted pattern is }(LL),\\
1,&\text{if the lifted pattern is }(NN).
\end{cases}
\]
Equivalently, $\varepsilon(e)=0$ records trivial sheet holonomy across
the edge, while $\varepsilon(e)=1$ records nontrivial sheet exchange.
This $\mathbb{Z}_2$-valued edge function is called the \emph{Thalean cocycle}.
\end{definition}

\subsection{Cocycle theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Thalean cocycle theorem]
The edge function
\[
\varepsilon : E(G_{15}) \to \mathbb{Z}_2
\]
defined above has the following properties:

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
\item It is canonically induced by the lifted transport
$G_{30}\to G_{15}$.

\item It records whether transport across a core edge preserves or swaps
sheet in the double cover.

\item Its cycle parity computes the sheet holonomy of lifted loops:
for any cycle $C\subset G_{15}$,
\[
\omega(C):=\sum_{e\in C}\varepsilon(e)\pmod 2
\]
is trivial if and only if the lifted loop closes on the same sheet.

\item It defines a nontrivial cohomology class in
$H^1(G_{15},\mathbb{Z}_2)$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The lift $G_{30}\to G_{15}$ provides a canonical two-sheet structure over
each vertex of $G_{15}$. For each base edge, the induced adjacency between
the two fibers is either sheet-preserving or sheet-swapping. This binary
distinction is invariant under exchanging the two labels in a fiber, so
it defines a well-defined element of $\mathbb{Z}_2$ on each edge.

Under the canonical identification of $G_{15}$ with the graph of opposite
edge-pairs of the dodecahedron, the two lifted realizations of each base
edge are found to be simultaneously local or simultaneously nonlocal,
yielding exactly the patterns $(LL)$ and $(NN)$. Thus the cocycle admits
the stated geometric interpretation.

For a cycle $C$ in $G_{15}$, the parity sum of edge values records whether
the lifted walk in $G_{30}$ returns to its original sheet or to the opposite
sheet. Hence the cocycle parity computes the sheet holonomy of the loop.
Computational verification shows that this class is nontrivial.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Holonomy classes of pentagons}

The cocycle does not create the pentagons of $G_{15}$; rather, it
polarizes them into trivial and nontrivial holonomy classes. In
particular, the parity sum
\[
\omega(C)=\sum_{e\in C}\varepsilon(e)\pmod 2
\]
distinguishes pentagons whose lifted transport closes trivially from
those carrying nontrivial sheet-flip holonomy.

\subsection{Signed-lift interpretation}

If one passes from $\mathbb{Z}_2=\{0,1\}$ to $\{\pm1\}$ by the rule
\[
\sigma(e)=(-1)^{\varepsilon(e)},
\]
then $\sigma(e)=+1$ corresponds to trivial holonomy and
$\sigma(e)=-1$ to nontrivial holonomy. Thus the Thalean cocycle may be
viewed equivalently as a signed edge function on $G_{15}$ or as the
$\mathbb{Z}_2$-voltage defining the double cover $G_{30}\to G_{15}$.

This cocycle is the fundamental transport invariant of the Petersen
core and serves as the algebraic input for the sector incidence
construction developed in the following sections.
