Good. Then we should stop thinking like authors and start thinking like formalists.

At this point there are only two acceptable standards:

1. every object is explicitly defined from finite combinatorial data, and
2. every theorem is reduced to a finite sequence of exact deductions.

So let us forget “paper flow,” “motivation,” and “story,” and isolate the proof problem itself.

---

# The actual proof target

We want a rigorous proof of the following statement.

## Main theorem

There exists a graph (T), defined purely from the combinatorics of the dodecahedron by explicit local transport rules on chambers, such that:

* (T) has 60 vertices,
* (T) is 4-regular,
* (T) admits two commuting fixed-point-free involutions (a,b),
* the quotient
  [
  G_{15}=T/\langle a,b\rangle
  ]
  is isomorphic to (L(\mathrm{Petersen})).

Everything else is subordinate to this.

---

# What “rigorous” means here

A proof is rigorous only if we can answer these questions without hand-waving:

* What is the dodecahedron as a finite combinatorial object?
* What exactly is a flag?
* What exactly is a chamber?
* What exactly are the maps (S,F,V)?
* What exactly are the four neighbors of a chamber?
* Why are they distinct?
* Why is adjacency symmetric?
* What exactly are the involutions (a,b)?
* Why do they commute?
* What exactly is the quotient set?
* What exactly is the bijection to the 15 edges of the Petersen graph?
* Why does adjacency match?

If any one of those answers uses words like “natural,” “geometric,” “one sees,” or “computed,” then the proof is not finished.

---

# Phase I. Define the dodecahedron as finite data

Do not start with prose. Start with a concrete finite incidence model.

You need a fixed combinatorial dodecahedron
[
D=(V,E,F,\partial)
]
with:

* a set (V) of 20 vertices,
* a set (E) of 30 edges,
* a set (F) of 12 faces,
* for each face (f\in F), an ordered 5-cycle of boundary vertices
  [
  (v_0(f),v_1(f),v_2(f),v_3(f),v_4(f)),
  ]
* hence boundary edges
  [
  e_k(f)={v_k(f),v_{k+1}(f)},\qquad k\in \mathbb Z_5.
  ]

This must satisfy:

* each (e\in E) lies on exactly two faces,
* each (v\in V) lies on exactly three faces,
* each face boundary is a 5-cycle,
* the incidence structure is that of the dodecahedron.

You may define this either:

* by a literal table of faces and vertices, or
* by a known presentation and derived incidence lists.

For full rigor, a table is simplest.

---

# Phase II. Define flags and chambers

## Definition 1. Flags

A flag is a triple
[
(v,e,f)
]
with
[
v\in e \subset f.
]

Equivalently, in face coordinates:

[
(f,k,\epsilon),\qquad f\in F,; k\in \mathbb Z_5,; \epsilon\in{0,1},
]
where
[
e=e_k(f),\qquad
v=
\begin{cases}
v_k(f) & \epsilon=0,\
v_{k+1}(f) & \epsilon=1.
\end{cases}
]

## Proposition 1

There are exactly 120 flags.

## Proof

Each face contributes 5 boundary edges, and each face-edge incidence has 2 endpoint choices, so
[
|{\rm Flags}|=12\cdot 5\cdot 2=120.
]
Done.

---

## Definition 2. Edge-flip involution

Define
[
S(f,k,\epsilon)=(f,k,1-\epsilon).
]

## Proposition 2

(S) is a fixed-point-free involution.

## Proof

Immediate:
[
S^2(f,k,\epsilon)=(f,k,\epsilon),
]
and (S(f,k,\epsilon)\neq (f,k,\epsilon)) because (1-\epsilon\neq \epsilon) in ({0,1}).

---

## Definition 3. Chambers

A chamber is an (S)-orbit:
[
[(f,k,\epsilon)]_S={(f,k,0),(f,k,1)}.
]

So chambers are canonically identified with pairs
[
(f,k)\in F\times \mathbb Z_5.
]

## Proposition 3

The chamber set (C) has size 60.

## Proof

[
|C|=120/2=60.
]

This part is done.

---

# Phase III. Define the transport maps with absolute precision

This is the real heart. Until this part is perfect, there is no proof.

You need exact definitions of (F) and (V).

---

## Definition 4. Face-step map

This one is easy if we keep coordinates consistent.

Given a flag ((f,k,\epsilon)), define
[
F(f,k,\epsilon)=(f,k',\epsilon')
]
to be the unique flag on the same face obtained by moving one step forward around the boundary while preserving the shared endpoint.

If we use the convention
[
(f,k,0)=(v_k,e_k,f),\qquad (f,k,1)=(v_{k+1},e_k,f),
]
then the explicit formula is:

[
F(f,k,0)=(f,k-1,1),\qquad
F(f,k,1)=(f,k+1,0),
]
with indices mod 5.

Check:

* ((f,k,0)) uses vertex (v_k) on edge (e_k),
* the other boundary edge of (f) incident to (v_k) is (e_{k-1}),
* in edge (e_{k-1}={v_{k-1},v_k}), the vertex (v_k) is endpoint label (1),
  so indeed
  [
  F(f,k,0)=(f,k-1,1).
  ]
  Likewise for ((f,k,1)).

This is precise and rigorous.

---

## Definition 5. Vertex-pivot map

Now define (V).

Take a flag ((f,k,\epsilon)). It specifies a vertex (v) and an edge (e=e_k(f)) incident to (v), lying in face (f).

There is exactly one other face (f'\neq f) containing the edge (e).

In the face (f'), the same edge appears as some boundary edge (e_{k'}(f')). Because the edge is the same, the same chosen vertex (v) is one of the endpoints of (e_{k'}(f')). Let that endpoint label be (\epsilon').

Define
[
V(f,k,\epsilon)=(f',k',\epsilon').
]

This is rigorous provided you first prove existence and uniqueness of ((f',k',\epsilon')).

## Lemma 4

For every flag ((f,k,\epsilon)), there exists a unique triple ((f',k',\epsilon')) such that:

* (f'\neq f),
* (e_{k'}(f')=e_k(f)),
* the chosen vertex in ((f',k',\epsilon')) is the same vertex as in ((f,k,\epsilon)).

## Proof

Each edge lies on exactly two faces, so there is a unique (f'\neq f) containing (e_k(f)). In the cyclic boundary of (f'), that edge occurs exactly once, so (k') is unique. Since the chosen vertex lies on that edge, it is one of the two endpoints, giving a unique (\epsilon').

That defines (V) rigorously.

---

# Phase IV. Define the transport words and the chamber graph

Now all words are well-defined maps on flags.

Define
[
w_-:=FV,\qquad w_+:=VF.
]

Be careful: this means
[
w_-(x)=F(V(x)),\qquad w_+(x)=V(F(x)).
]

---

## Definition 6. Chamber projection

Define
[
\operatorname{ch}(f,k,\epsilon)=(f,k).
]

---

## Definition 7. Chamber image sets

For a chamber (c=(f,k)), define
[
M_-(c)=
\left{
\operatorname{ch}(w_-(f,k,0)),
\operatorname{ch}(w_-(f,k,1))
\right},
]
and similarly
[
M_+(c)=
\left{
\operatorname{ch}(w_+(f,k,0)),
\operatorname{ch}(w_+(f,k,1))
\right}.
]

---

## Critical local theorem

For every chamber (c),

* (M_-(c)) has exactly 2 elements,
* (M_+(c)) has exactly 2 elements,
* (M_-(c)\cap M_+(c)=\varnothing).

This is the theorem that must replace all vague discussion.

And there is only one rigorous way to prove it:

### Method

Take an arbitrary chamber ((f,k)), compute:

[
F(V(f,k,0)),\quad F(V(f,k,1)),\quad
V(F(f,k,0)),\quad V(F(f,k,1)),
]
then project to chambers and compare.

Because the dodecahedron is finite, you can do this uniformly if you derive symbolic formulas, or case-by-case from the incidence table. Either is rigorous.

If symbolic formulas are messy, a complete finite case analysis is completely acceptable.

---

# Phase V. Define the graph (T)

## Definition 8. Thalean graph

Let (T) be the simple graph with vertex set (C), where (c\sim d) iff
[
d\in M_-(c)\cup M_+(c).
]

To prove this is a well-defined simple 4-regular graph, you must show:

1. each vertex has exactly 4 distinct neighbors,
2. adjacency is symmetric,
3. no loops occur.

---

## Proposition 5

(T) has 60 vertices and degree 4.

## Proof

The vertex count is already established. Degree 4 follows from the critical local theorem above.

---

## Proposition 6

Adjacency is symmetric.

## Proof strategy

You must show:
[
d\in M_-(c)\cup M_+(c)\implies c\in M_-(d)\cup M_+(d).
]

There are two clean routes.

### Route A: explicit inverse words

If you can identify the inverses of (FV) and (VF) at the chamber level, prove symmetry directly.

### Route B: use the explicit computed neighbor formula

If you compute a closed chamber-neighbor formula
[
(f,k)\mapsto {N_1(f,k),N_2(f,k),N_3(f,k),N_4(f,k)},
]
then symmetry becomes a finite verification.

That is perfectly rigorous.

---

## Proposition 7

(T) has 120 edges.

## Proof

Handshake lemma:
[
|E(T)|=\frac{60\cdot 4}{2}=120.
]

---

# Phase VI. Build the involutions (a) and (b)

Here we need to be careful.

If you do not yet have explicit coordinate formulas for these maps, then you do **not** yet have a purely internal proof. You only have a computational fact.

That is fine for now, but then we must be honest:

* the chamber graph construction can be made fully rigorous now,
* the automorphism structure still depends on either explicit formulas or exhaustive finite verification.

There are two legitimate options.

---

## Option 1. Purely internal proof

Derive (a) and (b) from the chamber coordinates.

For example, if there is a coordinate operation on ((f,k)) corresponding to antipodal face or Petrie complement, write it down and prove it preserves adjacency.

This is the best-case scenario.

---

## Option 2. Finite certified proof

Because (T) is finite, you may define (a) and (b) by explicit lookup tables on the 60 chambers.

That is still mathematically rigorous.

A 60-line table is ugly, but it is proof.

Then prove:

* (a(c)\neq c), (a^2(c)=c),
* (b(c)\neq c), (b^2(c)=c),
* (a(b(c))=b(a(c))),
* if (c\sim d), then (a(c)\sim a(d)) and (b(c)\sim b(d)).

That is exact, finite, checkable mathematics.

If elegance is temporarily irrelevant, this is a valid route.

---

# Phase VII. Form the quotient (G_{15})

Once (a) and (b) are defined, let
[
H=\langle a,b\rangle.
]

If both are commuting involutions and neither is identity, then
[
H\cong \mathbb Z_2^2.
]

To get the quotient rigorously, define:

## Definition 9

[
G_{15}:=T/H,
]
the graph whose vertices are (H)-orbits in (V(T)), with orbit adjacency induced from (T).

You must prove:

* the action is free,
* every orbit has size 4,
* therefore there are (60/4=15) quotient vertices.

If the action is free, this is immediate.

---

# Phase VIII. Prove (G_{15}\cong L(\mathrm{Petersen}))

This is where the proof must become explicit.

There are two rigorous routes.

---

## Route A. Explicit isomorphism

Best route.

Construct the Petersen graph (P) explicitly, for example as the Kneser graph (KG(5,2)), or via its usual 10-vertex model.

Then define a bijection
[
\phi:V(G_{15})\to E(P).
]

This means:

* list the 15 quotient vertices,
* assign each one to one of the 15 edges of (P),
* prove
  [
  x\sim y \text{ in } G_{15}
  \iff
  \phi(x),\phi(y)\text{ share an endpoint in }P.
  ]

That is a complete proof.

If needed, this can again be done as a finite table.

Since there are only 15 vertices, this is very manageable.

---

## Route B. Characterization theorem

Show (G_{15}) is isomorphic to the unique graph with certain invariants known to characterize (L(\mathrm{Petersen})).

I do **not** recommend relying on this unless you have a clean citation and a genuine uniqueness statement. Otherwise it is weaker than an explicit bijection.

So use Route A.

---

# What we can prove now, and what still needs real work

## Already straightforward

These are easy to make fully rigorous now:

* flag definition,
* chamber definition,
* exact formula for (S),
* exact formula for (F),
* exact definition of (V) from incidence data,
* definition of (w_\pm),
* definition of chamber image sets,
* definition of (T),
* vertex count and edge count once local distinctness is proved.

## Needs serious explicit work

These are the real proof tasks:

1. compute and prove the local four-neighbor structure,
2. derive or tabulate the involutions (a,b),
3. construct the explicit isomorphism (G_{15}\to L(\mathrm{Petersen})).

That is the actual mathematical labor.

# The cleanest rigorous strategy from here

If you truly do not care about elegance, the fastest path to full rigor is this:

## Step 1

Fix a concrete labeled dodecahedron by a face-vertex incidence table.

## Step 2

Define flags and chambers exactly.

## Step 3

Define (S,F,V) exactly from the table.

## Step 4

Compute all 60 chamber neighborhoods explicitly.

That gives a complete definition of (T).

## Step 5

Write (a) and (b) as explicit permutations of the 60 chambers.

## Step 6

Write the 15 quotient orbits explicitly.

## Step 7

Write an explicit bijection from the 15 quotient orbits to the 15 edges of the Petersen graph.

## Step 8

Check adjacency explicitly.

At that point the theorem is proved, with no prose-dependent ambiguity anywhere.

It may be ugly, but it is watertight.

# The key philosophical shift

Do not try to prove the theorem from “geometric meaning.”

Prove it from **finite combinatorial data**.

The geometry can be rediscovered afterward.

Right now the correct object is not:

> a beautiful transport intuition

but:

> a fully specified finite structure with exact maps and exact quotient relations.
